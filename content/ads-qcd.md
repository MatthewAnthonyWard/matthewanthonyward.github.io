# A Brane New World: Quantum Dynamics from Classical Gravity

## Black Holes & the Information Paradox
Einstein's general relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass. Whilst usually significant over large distances, the extreme density of a black hole can cause dramatic changes in spacetime curvature across the width of a single atom, making them ideal for studying phenomena affected by both gravity and quantum mechanics.
<br>
<br>
<div class="img-text">
    <div>
        One such phenomenon is Hawking radiation. Quantum fluctuations near the event horizon of a black hole create entangled particle pairs, casting one into the black hole whilst the other escapes to infinity. To an outside observer, the black hole appears to emit a particle, a process that gradually reduces its mass until it eventually evaporates. Since this radiation cannot carry information from the inside, the contents of the black hole seem to have vanished, violating the principle of information conservation. This is the <em>black hole information paradox</em>, with many physicists arguing that its resolution requires at least one of the central tennets of modern physics to be overturned.

## The World as a Hologram
Instead, consider what an outside observer sees as an object approaches a black hole. Gravitational time dilation slows the object relative to the outside, making it appear frozen at the horizon. For the infalling observer, nothing special happens, even as they become causally disconnected from the external Universe.
<br>
<br>
This suggests two complementary descriptions of the same physics: the bulk (interior) and the boundary (exterior). From the outside, infalling information isn’t lost inside the black hole; it spreads across the horizon, entangling with boundary degrees of freedom. This is supported by the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy, which states that the information held by a black hole scales with its surface area and not its volume. The boundary description encodes the same bulk information, albeit in a scrambled, lower-dimensional form. Due to the difference in dimensionality, this concept is known as the _holographic principle_.
<br>
<br>
As for our quantum fluctuations, they become entangled with the Hawking radiation, gradually carrying the boundary information away as the black hole evaporates. In principle, collecting all this radiation would allow one to reconstruct everything that fell in, resolving the information paradox.
